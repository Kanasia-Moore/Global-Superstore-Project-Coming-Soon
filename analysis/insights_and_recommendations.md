
## 1. Profit loss becomes more visible at 30% discounts or higher

Discount behavior shows that profitability weakens once discounts reach approximately 30% or more.

This indicates that the business may have a discount threshold where promotions begin to reduce margin instead of supporting profitable sales growth.

**Why this matters:**  
Stakeholders should avoid evaluating discount success by sales volume alone. A discount may increase order activity while still reducing overall profitability.

**Recommendation:**  
Review discount policies at or above the 30% threshold and compare whether those discounts generate enough sales lift to justify the profit loss.

---

## 2. Tables are the weakest-performing sub-category by profit

Tables produce the lowest overall profit among the sub-categories reviewed.

This makes Tables a priority area for deeper investigation because the issue may be tied to pricing, cost structure, shipping costs, discounting, or return behavior.

**Why this matters:**  
A product category can appear valuable from a sales perspective while still weakening business performance if margins are consistently negative or low.

**Recommendation:**  
Evaluate Tables separately from the broader Furniture category to determine whether pricing, discount limits, supplier costs, or fulfillment costs need to be adjusted.

---

## 3. Tables are commonly discounted around the 30% mark

The discount pattern for Tables is especially important because Tables are already the lowest-profit sub-category and are often discounted near the level where profit loss begins to appear.

This creates a compounding issue: a weak-margin product is being discounted at a level that may make profitability even harder to recover.

**Why this matters:**  
Discounting low-margin products too aggressively can create the appearance of sales activity while quietly reducing business health.

**Recommendation:**  
Set stricter discount controls for Tables and monitor whether reduced discounting improves profit margin without significantly damaging sales volume.

---

## 4. Strong revenue does not always mean strong profit

The dashboard shows visible gaps between revenue and profit across sub-categories. Some products generate meaningful revenue but do not produce proportional profit.

This reinforces the main business problem: sales alone can create a misleading view of performance.

**Why this matters:**  
Stakeholders need to evaluate revenue and profit together to avoid investing attention in areas that look successful but may not be financially efficient.

**Recommendation:**  
Use profit margin, discount band, and return behavior alongside sales when identifying high-priority categories.

---

## Summary Recommendation

Global Superstore should review its discount strategy with special attention to the 30%+ discount range and the Tables sub-category.

A stronger performance monitoring process should track:

- Sales
- Profit
- Profit margin
- Discount level
- Return behavior
- Sub-category performance

This would help stakeholders identify where revenue is not translating into sustainable profit and prevent discounting from masking deeper profitability issues.
